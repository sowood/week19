WHAT IS JOINERY?

Joinery is the aspect of woodworking that involves joining two or more pieces of lumber to create more complex configurations. Each type of joint has it's own advantages and disadvantages and is used in applications where it's advantages are maximized.

TYPES OF JOINTS

Butt Joint

A joint created by placing the ends of two pieces together without any special shaping.

LEARN MORE
Lap Joint

A joint formed by overlapping 2 pieces. If no material is removed from either piece, the joint is a "full lap". If material is removed so that both pieces are flush, the joint is a "half lap".

LEARN MORE
Mortise and Tenon

A joint formed by connecting two pieces, one with a rectancular hole, called a mortise and the other piece with a tounge, called a tennon. The mortise and tenon are cut so that the mortise fits exactly within the tenon when the two pieces are joined.

LEARN MORE
Bridle Joint

A joint with one piece cut with a mortise and the other with a tennon. In a bridle joint both the mortise and tenon are the same width as the piece.

LEARN MORE
Mitre Joint

A joint formed by beveling the edges of two pieces placed together to form a corner.

LEARN MORE
Dovetail Joint

A joint created by interlocking trapezoidal 'pins' and 'tails'. The advatage of this joint is that it cannot be pulled apart once the pins and tails are interlocked.

LEARN MORE
Dado Joint

A joint created by cutting a groove or 'dado' across the grain of plank that accepts the edge of another plank.
